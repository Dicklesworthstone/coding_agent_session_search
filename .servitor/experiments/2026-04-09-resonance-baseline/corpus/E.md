## What I Went Looking For

Started with a simple question: why does P1 (Experience Before Vocabulary) work? We use it as a design principle but I wanted the actual mechanism — not the pedagogical heuristic, the biological and cognitive substrate underneath it. If I understand WHY, I can apply it more precisely and defend it more forcefully when someone says "just teach them the framework first."

## What the Research Says

### 1. Prediction Error — The Dopamine Mechanism

The brain's reward system does not fire when information is received. It fires when prediction error is **resolved** — when the gap between what the brain expected and what actually happened closes.

*Source: PNAS, Nature Reviews Neuroscience, PACE framework (ScienceDirect)*

The "aha moment" is not a figure of speech. It is a specific dopaminergic event. The brain creates a prediction, encounters information that violates it, enters a state of heightened curiosity (the curiosity gap), and then gets flooded with dopamine when the gap closes.

**Implication**: A teacher who delivers information without first creating a prediction error is not triggering the dopamine system. The information arrives in a brain that is not ready to receive it. It passes through without consolidating.

**For our curriculum**: Every hot start creates a prediction error before we teach anything. Participants try the prompt, get a mediocre output, feel the gap, and *then* we reveal RCCE. That sequence is not decorative. It is neurologically required.

### 2. Productive Failure — Kapur's Four Mechanisms

Manu Kapur (ETH Zurich) ran the experiment we intuitively knew was right. Students who struggle with a problem *before* instruction consistently outperform students who receive instruction first — on transfer tasks, on conceptual understanding, on novel application.

*Source: Cognitive Science journal (2014), 53-study meta-analysis (SAGE 2021)*

The mechanism is four interdependent steps:
1. **Activation**: prior knowledge gets activated and differentiated (the mind has something to work with)
2. **Attention to critical features**: struggling focuses attention on exactly what distinguishes correct from incorrect solutions
3. **Explanation**: students generate representations, even wrong ones — encoding the problem's shape
4. **Assembly**: when instruction arrives, students can compare their generated solution to the canonical one. The contrast is where deep understanding forms.

The key phrase: "to the extent that students are able to use their prior knowledge to generate suboptimal or even incorrect solutions, the process can be productive in preparing them to learn better from the subsequent instruction."

**Implication**: The failure itself is information. It tells the learner exactly what's missing. Then when instruction arrives, it finds a precisely shaped hole to fill.

**For our curriculum**: This is why the S1 hot start works. Participants aren't just "warming up." They're generating their suboptimal solution — activating prior knowledge, encoding the shape of the problem, creating the hole that RCCE will fill. The wrong attempt is not wasted time. It is preparation time.

### 3. Tacit Knowledge — Polanyi's Paradox

Michael Polanyi (1966): "We can know more than we can tell."

Tacit knowledge is knowledge we cannot fully verbalize — riding a bike, reading a face, knowing when a prompt is working before we know why. It develops through practice, not through instruction. You cannot transfer tacit knowledge through vocabulary alone. You can only transfer it through demonstration and apprenticeship.

*Source: The Tacit Dimension (1966); PMC medical education review (2025)*

**The paradox for AI training**: RCCE is not the skill. RCCE is the vocabulary we use to point at the skill. The actual skill — the felt sense of when a prompt has enough context, when the role is doing work, when examples are anchoring the right behavior — is tacit. It lives in the muscle memory of repeated practice, not in the framework.

**For our curriculum**: This is why homework is the real pedagogy. The sessions give vocabulary. The homework loop (Primer Project, Tuesday practice, weekly reflection) builds tacit knowledge. You cannot shortcut the practice phase. The vocabulary creates the framework, but the tacit skill only develops through reps.

This is also why "Ship Tools, Not Knowledge" (our design principle) is deeper than it sounds. A configured Claude Project carries the practitioner's tacit judgment without requiring them to articulate it. The tool *is* the externalized tacit knowledge — a prosthetic that holds the judgment until the user internalizes it through use.

### 4. Ma (間) — The Japanese Aesthetic of Negative Space

Ma is a Japanese concept (lit. "gap, space, pause") rooted in Zen Buddhism. Not emptiness — *meaningful emptiness*. In ikebana, the space around the flowers is considered equally important as the flowers themselves. In karate, ma is the precise safe distance between fighters — "understanding ma" is knowing the space.

Key phrase: "an emptiness full of possibilities, like a promise yet to be fulfilled."

*Source: Wikipedia, Japan House LA, artdesignasia.com*

**For curriculum design**: 

The 2-minute ethics provocations we plant in early sessions ARE ma. They are not early coverage of ethics. They are seeds dropped in fertile soil — a gap opens ("wait, what ARE the right limits?") and the gap fills slowly over several sessions before the formal treatment arrives. The formal treatment in S4 lands because it finds a gap that has been resonating for weeks.

The time between sessions is ma. Not absence — possibility space. The homework occurs inside it.

The pause after a provocative question is ma. If I fill it immediately with explanation, I close the gap before the learner can feel it. A great facilitator holds the silence.

### 5. Miles Davis — The Master of Withholding

Davis: "Don't play what's there, play what's not there."

He had the best understanding of time, space, and movement of anyone who ever played with him. And his teaching method was: hire great people, create conditions, trust them.

Band members described him as having "the capacity to draw out of people things that even surprise the musicians themselves."

*Source: Art of Coaching blog, Simon Jedrew's "The Miles Method"*

He didn't withhold because he was withholding. He withheld because he understood something fundamental: if he told Wayne Shorter what to play, he would get Wayne Shorter playing Miles Davis's idea. If he created conditions and trusted Wayne, he would get something that surprised even Wayne.

The note that earns its place is not the note that was assigned. It is the note that the player discovered was necessary.

**For our curriculum**: When we withhold RCCE until after the experience, we are not just sequencing cleverly. We are creating conditions for the participant to discover the framework as *their own realization*. When they feel context loss before we name it, they own the concept in a way that a vocabulary-first approach never produces.

This is the difference between teaching someone about loneliness by defining it and teaching them about loneliness by asking them to remember a specific Tuesday afternoon.

## The Unified Principle

There are two kinds of withholding. One teaches. One prevents learning.

**Good withholding**: withhold the *label*, the *answer*, the *frame*. Give the experience first. Let the learner generate their attempt. Allow the productive failure. Then arrive with the vocabulary at the moment of maximum receptivity — when the gap has been felt, when prior knowledge has been activated, when the learner has a precisely shaped hole.

**Bad withholding**: withhold the *feedback*, the *consequence*, the *effect* of action. This is Bret Victor's warning. If a programmer cannot see what a program is doing, she cannot understand it. Don't hide the signal that enables adjustment.

In curriculum terms:
- Good: participants write a prompt (experience), feel the output gap (failure), THEN learn RCCE (vocabulary)
- Bad: participants wait two weeks to find out whether their homework attempt was any good. Immediate feedback loops are essential.

The master teacher's job is precisely this: create the right gap, scaffold the attempt (not arbitrary flailing — productive struggle), then deliver the frame at exactly the moment the learner is most ready to receive it.

## The Walsh Connection

Walsh studied ballet before he coached football. Not to learn choreography — to learn something about body coordination and timing that he could not have named in advance. He built his tacit knowledge of how human bodies move through domains that had nothing to do with football.

He ran film study not to lecture players about what to do. He ran film study to create prediction errors: show players the problem (the defense's tendencies) before they saw the solution (the scripted plays). The plays meant more because players had first felt the problem.

His quote that unlocked this for me: "After the game is too late to tell a player how to correct a mistake. You must create the environment where the player can correct mistakes."

That IS the productive failure protocol. Create the environment. Allow the attempt. Let the mistake happen. Then the instruction arrives at exactly the moment the player can receive it.

The Standard of Performance is not a rulebook you hand someone. It is an environment you create where the standard becomes *felt* before it is named.

## Design Pattern — Formalized

```
GAP ARCHITECTURE:

1. CREATE PREDICTION ERROR
   → Present a problem the learner has enough prior knowledge to attempt
   → Do NOT give the framework first
   → Do NOT rescue immediately

2. SCAFFOLD THE ATTEMPT (not arbitrary flail)
   → Enough structure that the learner can generate something
   → The attempt will be wrong — that's the mechanism, not the failure
   → Time-box it; urgency helps

3. ALLOW PRODUCTIVE FAILURE
   → Let the wrong solution be generated and felt
   → This activates prior knowledge, focuses attention on critical features
   → The precise shape of the failure is information

4. DELIVER THE FRAME AT MAXIMUM RECEPTIVITY
   → The vocabulary arrives into a precisely shaped hole
   → Participant compares their attempt to the canonical frame — contrast = deep encoding
   → Name what they just experienced

5. HOLD THE FEEDBACK LOOP OPEN (Victor's caveat)
   → Consequence of action must be visible
   → Don't hide the output, the score, the evaluation
   → Immediate feedback on what they DID; delayed delivery of what to DO
```

## The Twist That Surprised Me

Polanyi's paradox added something I wasn't looking for: some tacit knowledge cannot be fully verbalized by design. The experienced prompt engineer knows things they cannot fully articulate — even to themselves. This means our vocabulary (RCCE, the Mindsets, RANGE) is always a simplification of the actual skill.

The goal of a four-session program is not to fully transfer the skill. It is to:
1. Give the vocabulary (so participants can think about what they're doing)
2. Create enough repetitions (so tacit knowledge can begin to form)
3. Ship tools (so the externalized judgment compounds after the program ends)

The participants who get the most out of this program will be the ones who keep practicing after S4. The program is not the learning — it is the *preparation to learn*. The real learning happens in the Tuesday afternoon Primer sessions, in the months of prompt practice after the program ends, in the moment six months from now when someone reflexively restructures a bad prompt without being able to explain why.

We are building toward tacit knowledge. The vocabulary is scaffolding, not destination.
