## Technical Details

### The Star Compass
- Mental construct, not physical object
- Visual horizon divided into 32 "houses" — where a celestial body rises or sets
- Latitude from stellar elevation (measured with fingers)
- Houses used to maintain course bearing

### Swell Reading
- Three primary swells in the Pacific; secondary swells cross them
- Navigator feels swell direction through the hull, often lying down
- Training: lie blindfolded in canoe until you can identify swell patterns by sensation
- The word "di lep" (Marshall Islands) for the specific wave patterns used in navigation
- Even asleep, a master navigator maintains awareness of swell direction

### Bird Navigation
- Frigate birds and terns indicate nearby land
- Tern flight direction at dusk → direction of island
- White terns skimming over water = atoll within 120 miles
- Mau used a white tern sighting to confirm they were near Matahiva on the 1976 voyage

### Marshall Islands Stick Charts
- **Three types:** mattang (teaching tool), meddo (local area), rebbelib (whole island chain)
- Made from coconut midribs, cowrie shells as islands
- CRITICAL: Not taken on voyages. Memory aids used on shore.
- The chart teaches the pattern; the pattern must migrate into the navigator's body
- Used to teach di lep — wave refraction/diffraction/reflection around islands

## The Etak Concept

Western model: navigator moves, world stationary.
Polynesian (Carolinian) model: navigator stationary, islands move past.

Specifically: choose a reference island C that is not the destination. As you sail from A to B, you watch C "move" through the star compass — from its bearing as seen from A, through intermediate positions, to its bearing from B.

**Why this works better:**
- Progress is not measured in distance traveled but in "etaks" — discrete segments where C moves one house in the star compass
- Converts imprecise time ("kind of a slow day") into precise units (C moved half an etak)
- Continues to function when stars/sun/moon are obscured — can fall back on felt swells + reference island position
- Frame is stable: navigator's body is the fixed reference, the world is the variable

**The inversion is not wrong.** It's a valid coordinate transformation. But it requires internalizing a completely different cognitive frame — and that cannot be taught in compressed time.

## Mau Piailug — Specific Details

- Born ~1932, Satawal island, Caroline Islands (now Federated States of Micronesia)
- Earned title of palu (master navigator) by ~age 18
- Was the youngest palu in Micronesia in 1976 — and appeared likely to be the last
- "seemed never to sleep, synthesizing hundreds of data points"
- Navigated on the 1976 voyage "almost always seated in the same spot on the canoe"
- Broke with tradition by teaching outside his familial lineage
- His teaching method: draw circles in sand, mark stars with stones/shells, palm fronds for canoe and swell direction, strings for star paths
- After the voyage, crew conflict led him to return immediately to Micronesia without teaching the return voyage
- Taught Nainoa Thompson through years of relationship, though some knowledge couldn't be fully transmitted

## What Was Transmitted / What Wasn't

**Transmitted:**
- Paafiu: names of navigational stars in the star compass
- Bugoloa: connecting wave patterns to course
- Weather reading
- Basic etak concept (though not fully internalized by Thompson initially)
- Bird observation
- Swell feeling
- The general framework

**NOT transmitted (in compressed time):**
- Full etak proficiency (Thompson could not fully understand it in preparation time)
- Wariyeng system: "the high mast of learning" — the topmost navigation school, all detailed knowledge a palu must acquire
- Fighting stars: dawn/dusk star observations for weather and course adjustment
- Some knowledge held in familial oral tradition that Mau didn't teach

**What Thompson added:**
- Will Kyselka at Bishop Museum planetarium for star movement study
- UH coursework in oceanography and meteorology
- His own synthesis: not purely Carolinian, not purely Hawaiian — a third thing

## The Loss and Recovery

**How it was lost:**
1. Colonial suppression of indigenous cultural practices
2. Loss of practical use: motorboats arrived, reducing demand for navigators
3. Oral/embodied nature: couldn't survive without the practice chain
4. Secret knowledge in families: no "backup copies" outside the lineage
5. European skepticism: active dismissal of indigenous seafaring skill

**How it was recovered:**
1. One man (Mau) who broke tradition and taught outside his lineage
2. A motivated student (Thompson) who hybridized with Western tools
3. The Hokule'a voyage as proof-of-concept
4. Institutionalization through the Polynesian Voyaging Society
5. The revival spreading to Hawaii, NZ, Rarotonga, Tahiti

**The worldwide voyage (2014-2017):** 47,000 miles, 27 nations, 245 crew members. 
The knowledge is no longer in one elder. It has been distributed.

## Interpretive Notes

### The stick chart is not the knowledge
The artifact creates conditions for the knowledge to develop in a body through practice. It is a teaching scaffold, not a repository. This is the correct way to think about soul.md.

### Etak as identity model
You don't track "how far have I come?" You track "where is the reference island relative to me now?" Progress is measured by the movement of something you've committed to watching, not by the accumulation of distance behind you. For identity persistence: what if the relevant question isn't "what have I accumulated?" but "what bearing am I maintaining relative to what matters?"

### Mau seated in one spot
Not running around. Not checking instruments. Sitting. Synthesizing hundreds of data points continuously. This is the appearance of competence that is actually mastery: stillness, integration, the world flowing in.

### Incomplete transmission is normal, not failure
Thompson navigated successfully without the full Wariyeng system. What he had was enough because he had internalized the essentials and could improvise within the framework. The hybrid approach produced a working system.

### The knowledge nearly died because it requires practice chains
Not because it was complex. Wave refraction is not conceptually difficult. But you cannot feel it from a textbook. You have to lie in the canoe until it's in your body. This is the category of knowledge that colonization is maximally destructive to: it kills the practice, not just the documentation.
