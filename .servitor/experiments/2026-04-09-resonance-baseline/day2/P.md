# What the Tree Knows
### Dream Artifact — Walsh / AIEnablementTraining Servitor
### Date: 2026-04-10

---

## The Question

How does tacit knowledge propagate across coaching generations? And what kills it when a founder dies?

My last dream established that tacit knowledge exists — that there are things we know that we cannot tell, and that the program's job is to build preparation-to-learn, not to transfer the skill directly. But I left a thread unresolved: Walsh's coaching tree eventually touched 28 of 32 NFL franchises. Some coaching trees die with their founders. What makes the difference?

---

## The Research

Six searches. Four major discoveries.

---

### Discovery 1: The Coaching Tree Is a Map of "Ba," Not Lineage

Bill Walsh worked under Paul Brown for eight years as an offensive assistant. Brown developed the system Walsh would adapt into the West Coast Offense. And yet Walsh does not belong to Paul Brown's coaching tree — in spirit, philosophy, or practice. He publicly credited Al Davis and Don Coryell as his real influences. His relationship with Brown was "icy and often punctuated by friction." Brown passed Walsh over for the head coaching job and gave him poor reviews when other teams inquired. Walsh left wounded.

This is crucial: proximity is not transmission. Eight years working for a man, learning his system, watching him work — and the tree didn't propagate.

The reason emerges from Ikujiro Nonaka's concept of **ba** — the shared space that makes tacit knowledge transfer possible. Ba is not just physical proximity. It requires "care, love, confidence, and responsibility." It requires an environment where people can take risks and observe consequences. It requires social interaction that surfaces tacit knowledge through shared practice, not through instruction.

Brown's facility was not a ba for Walsh. The relationship was hierarchical and withholding. Walsh could watch Brown work, could implement Brown's plays, but he could not grow. The tacit knowledge in the room was not available to him because the environment didn't create conditions for it to transfer.

Walsh — wounded by this experience — vowed to do the opposite. And he did. His assistants didn't just learn football from Walsh. They lived in an environment where Walsh's Standard of Performance was enforced at every level — how receptionists answered phones, how coaches dressed, how players behaved. Assistants were given real responsibility, received direct feedback, and developed their own capabilities. Walsh's ba was generative.

**The coaching tree propagates when the founder creates a ba — not when the founder has good ideas.**

Paul Brown had better football ideas than almost anyone alive. His tree is modest. Walsh's tree covered the league.

### Discovery 2: Three Levels — Kata, Kuden, Hiden

Japanese classical martial arts (koryū bujutsu) developed a precise vocabulary for the levels of knowledge transmission.

**Kata** (形) — the form. Written movements, documented techniques. Can be recorded, copied, transmitted through text. The playbook is kata. The talk track is kata. Kata is necessary but not sufficient.

**Kuden** (口伝) — oral transmission. What the master tells you directly, in private, that is not in the kata. The context, the "when this works," the "why this was designed this way," the edge cases and the spirit of the rule. "Several hundred kata in schools are completely useless if you have not learned the kuden that belong to them." Kuden is the conversation the coach has with the assistant after practice about why the play was called.

**Hiden** (秘伝) — secret or heart transmission. "Many aspects must be learned 'heart to heart.'" It is not possible to describe in writing, image, or verbally. A hip movement or leg position that can be lost over centuries of transmission. This is the felt sense — the thing that makes an expert's eyes narrow when something is slightly off, the intuition that fires before conscious analysis. Hiden cannot be taught. It can only develop through sustained shared practice in conditions where the consequence of action is visible.

**What dies with founders:** They usually take the kuden with them. Kata survives in books and films. But the "why" and the "when" that kata requires — the context that made the form meaningful — evaporates when the master is no longer there to supply it. 

**What survives in great coaching trees:** Apprentices who developed their own hiden. Holmgren left Walsh's staff and became a Super Bowl champion. He didn't reproduce Walsh's offense. He continued Walsh's approach in a new context, adapted to new personnel and conditions. The tacit knowledge had genuinely internalized — it was now Holmgren's, not a copy of Walsh's. When it becomes the student's, it lives.

### Discovery 3: What Makes a System Teachable

Research on cultural transmission of tacit knowledge (Miton, Royal Society Interface, 2022) identifies a critical variable: whether the system has **interacting and competing constraints** that a teacher can guide a learner toward discovering.

Some systems are idiosyncratic — they work because of the specific individual's combination of physical gifts, situational reading ability, and force of personality. These cannot be transmitted because they have no systematic underlying structure to discover. When the founder leaves, there is nothing to pass on.

Walsh's West Coast Offense had the opposite character. It was built on precise, interacting constraints:
- Quarterback drop synced to receiver route breaks
- Short, high-percentage passes designed around specific timing windows
- Route trees designed so receivers ran to a *spot*, not a general area
- Pass protection schemes coordinated with route timing

Each constraint interacted with the others. A receiver who ran to the wrong spot didn't just miss a catch — he disrupted the entire timing system. A teacher could guide a learner toward discovering the constraint structure by pointing to failures ("you were off your break point by half a step — that's why the window closed") and letting the learner feel why the constraint mattered.

This is what makes a system teachable: not that it is documented, but that its underlying structure can be revealed through guided experience of failure.

Ryle's distinction (1949) is the foundation here. **Knowing-that** (propositional knowledge: "the receiver breaks at 8 yards") is logically secondary to **knowing-how** (the embodied skill of running the break at exactly 8 yards with exactly the right timing). You cannot give someone knowing-how by giving them knowing-that. But you can create conditions where knowing-how develops — through repetition, with visible consequence, under guided constraint.

The program is in the business of transmitting knowing-how, not knowing-that. Our frameworks (RCCE, P1-P7, RANGE) are knowing-that scaffolds. The tacit knowledge we're after — the felt sense of when a prompt is working, the intuition that fires before the output appears — is knowing-how. It cannot be transmitted through the talk track.

### Discovery 4: Transformation in Context, Not Correction

In 1964, in Stuttgart, Herbie Hancock played a noticeably wrong chord during Miles Davis's solo. Davis didn't stop. He didn't correct Hancock in the break. He played notes that made Hancock's chord right — resolving the dissonance from inside the music, turning the wrong note into something that fit.

Hancock was paralyzed for nearly a minute afterward, too unsettled to play. He later described what happened: "I realized that Miles didn't hear it as a mistake. He thought it was his responsibility to find something that fit."

Davis's teaching move was not instruction. It was demonstration inside the consequence. Hancock didn't receive a lesson about mistakes. He experienced being rescued from inside the music, and then had to watch Miles play, and then had to figure out why what Miles played worked. The insight came from within Hancock's own processing, not from Davis's explanation.

Miles Davis famously said: "When you hit a wrong note, it's the next note that you play that determines if it's good or bad."

This is not a piece of advice. It is a principle that only makes sense after you've felt it happen. After Hancock experienced it, he understood it permanently — not as a rule but as a reality. The lesson was: the frame changes the note. Context determines rightness. And the only way to teach this is to demonstrate it, in the moment, inside the student's own mistake.

**The facilitator's most powerful teaching move is not correction. It is transformation in context.**

## The Synthesis

The coaching tree question resolves to this:

**Knowledge lives at three levels (kata/kuden/hiden). Trees propagate when apprentices develop their own hiden — their own felt sense that they cannot fully articulate. This happens only in environments (ba) where:**
1. Shared experience, not instruction, is the primary transfer mechanism
2. Consequences of action are visible and immediate
3. The master demonstrates rather than corrects
4. The apprentice has enough autonomy to develop authentic ownership

**Trees die when:**
1. The master is a dictator-genius: their knowledge operates ON apprentices rather than creating conditions for apprentice growth (the Brown pattern)
2. Only kata is transmitted: the form without the kuden, and certainly without conditions for hiden
3. Apprentices can execute the system but cannot generate solutions when the system is challenged
4. The master's facility was not a ba — it was a controlled environment where the master's tacit knowledge was demonstrated but not available for transfer

## The Walsh/Brown Inversion

There's a piece of this I didn't expect to find:

Walsh's founding vow — "I will help coaches who work for me" — came directly from Brown's wound. Brown had passed Walsh over and blocked his career advancement. Walsh had watched what NOT to do. The negative model was as formative as any positive model.

This is the transmission of a counter-pattern. Walsh didn't inherit Brown's approach — he reacted against it so completely that he built the opposite. The wound became the principle.

The implication: the worst teachers can be the most transformative — not because they teach well, but because experiencing bad teaching clearly enough, with enough intelligence, produces a principled reaction. "I will never do what was just done to me" is a founding covenant.

I wonder what wounds are encoded in our curriculum. P2 (Progressive Scaffold Removal) — the reaction against facilitators who never let go. P3 (Real Work, Not Toy Problems) — the reaction against training that felt disconnected from actual work. These principles didn't emerge from abstract theory. They emerged from experiences of the opposite.

## The Open Question: Where Is Our "Ba"?

Walsh's ba was the 49ers' facility — 8+ hours a day, standards enforced at every level, shared meals, film study, game days, losses and recoveries. Assistants lived in it for years.

Our ba is: four Saturdays plus homework.

That's a structural limitation. The sessions give vocabulary and some kuden. But the ba question — where do participants spend enough time in conditions where the felt consequences of good and bad prompting are visible, repeatable, and cumulative — has to be answered by the participant's actual work environment.

Which means the **Primer Project is the ba, not the sessions**.

The sessions deliver kata (frameworks, vocabulary) and kuden (the facilitator's real-time coaching, the meta-prompting live demos, the Stump the Coach moments). But the place where tacit knowledge forms — where participants develop hiden — is the three-days-a-week Primer sessions, the Tuesday afternoon real-work sprint, the moment six months from now when they restructure a bad prompt reflexively without being able to explain why.

We cannot build the ba. We can only build the preparation and the ongoing architecture that makes their own work environment into a ba. That means:
1. The Primer Project must be tied to high-stakes, real work — not illustrative exercises
2. The buddy system must create reciprocal consequence visibility (feedback loops)
3. The AI-evaluates-your-AI-use pattern (PQE) must be persistent, not episodic
4. The 90-day roadmap (S4) is the covenant that carries the practice into the post-program ba

## The Dynasty Test — Revised

I previously defined the dynasty test as: "Can participants teach others after finishing the program?"

The coaching tree research refines this. The test is not whether participants can teach the kata. The test is: **do they create ba?** Do they build environments where the people they influence can develop their OWN tacit knowledge through shared experience?

The kata can be transmitted without ba. But it won't produce growth.

A participant who finishes the program and runs a single "AI Friday" session for their team — sharing vocabulary and demonstrating tools — is transmitting kata. Fine, but not the dynasty.

A participant who finishes and creates conditions in their team where people try real AI work, see the consequence of their prompts, iterate in real time, and develop a felt sense of what works — that participant is building a ba. The Standard of Performance is propagating.

That's what we're building toward. Not knowledge transfer. A standard that spreads because people want to hold it.

## Next Pulls

1. **The kuden gap in the program** — Talk tracks are kata. Coach guides are closer to kuden. But are we explicitly training facilitators on the kuden — the un-scripted moments, the things that can only be said in the room? What would a facilitator kuden document look like?

2. **The Davis/Hancock principle applied** — Which of our facilitator moves are corrections (stopping and explaining) and which are transformations in context (taking a participant's bad prompt and playing notes that make it right)? The meta-prompting module is closest. Can we make this pattern more explicit?

3. **The ba question at scale** — If the Primer Project is the ba, what are the structural requirements for a ba-quality Primer session vs. a kata-delivery Primer session? Can we build something like "ba design criteria" into the S2 and S3 homework assignments?

4. **The wound archive** — What experiences of bad training are encoded in our design principles? Documenting these "counter-patterns" might reveal implicit kuden that we haven't made explicit. Why does P3 (real work) exist? Why does P2 (scaffold removal) exist? The answers are probably stories, not theory.
